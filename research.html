Research

From polarised light physics to planetary atmosphere observations

I study how light is scattered, absorbed, reflected, and polarised by planetary atmospheres. My work combines first-principles radiative-transfer modelling with observations of Earth, Venus, and Earth-like exoplanets.

Methodological foundation

Polarised radiative transfer

The common thread in my research is the physics of reflected light. I develop and use radiative-transfer models that track how photons interact with molecules, aerosols, clouds, and surfaces, including the polarisation state of light.

This foundation is what connects my different application areas. The same physical ideas are needed to interpret TROPOMI measurements of clouds and aerosols, simulate Venus for EnVision, and predict ocean signatures in future exoplanet observations.

Research themes

Earth observation

Clouds, aerosols, shadows, and solar eclipses in satellite data

My Earth-observation work focuses on situations where the measured radiation field is shaped by three-dimensional cloud structure and changing illumination. These effects are often treated as complications in satellite retrievals, but they also contain physical information.

During my PhD at TU Delft and KNMI, I developed DARCLOS to detect cloud shadows in TROPOMI data and a method to restore top-of-atmosphere reflectances during solar eclipses. The eclipse correction made it possible to use eclipses as natural experiments, leading to the discovery that shallow cumulus clouds over land can dissipate rapidly when sunlight is partly reduced. This has implications for how clouds may respond to proposed sunlight-reduction climate-intervention ideas.

Venus

Spectropolarimetry for ESA's EnVision mission

Venus is a nearby laboratory for testing radiative transfer in a thick, cloudy planetary atmosphere. In my postdoctoral work, I am adapting MONKI for Venus by extending it toward spherical atmospheres, refraction, and challenging twilight and limb-viewing geometries.

The goal is to simulate spectra and polarisation for the VenSpec instruments onboard ESA's EnVision mission. These simulations help connect the fundamental physics of light scattering in Venus's atmosphere with future mission observations.

Earth-like exoplanets

Oceans and clouds in reflected starlight

Future telescopes will not resolve Earth-like exoplanets as images. Instead, they will measure faint reflected starlight from a single point. I study which signatures of oceans, clouds, and planetary surfaces survive in total flux, colour, and polarisation.

My exoplanet work predicted ways to detect liquid-water oceans using polarised reflected light. These simulations were later used in a study of how NASA's future space-telescope concept, the Habitable Worlds Observatory, could detect such signatures around other worlds.

Connecting idea

One physics problem, three planetary settings

Earth provides detailed observations for testing radiative-transfer ideas, Venus provides an extreme nearby atmosphere, and exoplanets provide the long-term motivation: interpreting weak reflected-light signals from distant worlds. Across these settings, the central question remains the same — what does measured light tell us about the atmosphere and surface that produced it?
